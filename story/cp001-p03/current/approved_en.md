# Oath of the Peach Garden

The shadow at the door became a man.

He pushed a wheelbarrow up to the tavern wall, leaned it against the bricks, and strode inside. He did not sit down. He called out to the barkeep:

"Wine. Fast. I'm heading into the city to enlist."

Liu Bei's cup stopped halfway to his mouth.

He looked at the man — half a head taller than Zhang Fei, nine chi at least. A beard hung to his chest, a good two chi long. His face was dark red, as if he had just walked out of the sun — except the color was not from the sun. His lips were full, his eyes narrow and tilted up at the corners, his eyebrows thick and heavy. Standing still, he looked like he was already watching you.

Liu Bei set down his cup and stood.

"Friend, would you sit and drink with us?"

The man looked at him. His gaze swept across Liu Bei's face, then across Zhang Fei's grin. He paused for two seconds. No ceremony — he pulled out a bench and sat.

"My name is Guan Yu. Courtesy name Yunchang. I'm from Xieliang in Hedong."

"Five years ago, a local tyrant was terrorizing people in my hometown. I killed him. I've been on the move ever since — five, six years. I heard they're recruiting men to fight the rebels here. So I came."

Zhang Fei slapped the table.

"Fight rebels? That's what we're doing! This is Liu Bei — imperial kin, a man with great ambition —"

Liu Bei gave Zhang Fei a look. Zhang Fei shut up, but he did not take back the grin.

Liu Bei explained his plan. Not many words — three sentences and the thing was clear: the empire is in chaos, he wants to defeat the rebels and bring peace, but his hands are empty.

Guan Yu listened. When Liu Bei finished, he nodded.

"Good."

One word. The hand holding his cup went still.

Zhang Fei stood up and slapped his thigh. "Come to my estate. We'll talk properly."

---

Zhang Fei's estate sat outside Zhuo County. It was not large, but behind it stretched a peach orchard.

This time of year, the blossoms were at their peak. Pink covered every branch, every tree. The wind blew and petals fell like snow.

Zhang Fei led the two men through the orchard and stopped in the center. He turned around. The grin was gone. What replaced it was something rare on his face — seriousness.

"I have an idea."

Liu Bei and Guan Yu both looked at him.

"Behind this estate, there's this orchard. Tomorrow, the three of us make an offering to heaven and earth. We become brothers."

He paused.

"From now on, we work together. We do something real. What do you say?"

Liu Bei did not answer right away. Neither did Guan Yu.

Zhang Fei's patience cracked. "Well? Yes or no, just —"

"Yes."

Liu Bei said it. Guan Yu nodded.

The next day, the peach blossoms were still open.

An altar stood in the orchard — a black ox, a white horse, incense, three sets of bowls and chopsticks. No musicians. No guests. Only petals drifting down onto the ash.

The three men knelt before the altar. Liu Bei in the center, Guan Yu on the left, Zhang Fei on the right.

Liu Bei spoke first:

"I, Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei — though not of the same blood, this day we become brothers —"

Their voices joined:

"We will stand together, protect the helpless, and serve the state. We will not ask to be born on the same day, but we swear to die on the same day. Let heaven and earth bear witness — whoever breaks this oath will be forsaken by gods and men alike."

Their voices were not loud, but in the empty orchard every word rang clear.

When it was done, they bowed. Rose. Looked at each other.

From that moment, Liu Bei was the eldest, Guan Yu the second, Zhang Fei the youngest.

Zhang Fei was the first to grin.

After the oath, Zhang Fei had an ox slaughtered and wine brought out. Young men from the surrounding villages heard the news and came — some knew how to fight, some had hunted, some just stayed for the spectacle. More than three hundred men packed into the peach orchard and drank through the night.

---

Dawn broke. Petals carpeted the ground, wine jars lay on their sides.

Three hundred men, assembled. But horses?

No horses meant no speed on the battlefield. Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei sat in the estate staring at their weapons. They had blades, they had bows. No mounts.

While they sat, word came from the gate: two horse merchants had arrived outside the estate with a large herd, asking to rest.

Liu Bei went to meet them.

Two middle-aged men, dressed for the road, with dozens of horses grazing behind them. They were Zhang Shiping and Su Shuang, big traders from Zhongshan who drove horses north every year. This year the north was overrun by bandits, so they turned back.

Liu Bei invited them in, poured wine, talked. He told them what he meant to do — fight the rebels, protect the people. No embellishment. Just the facts.

The two merchants exchanged a look.

Zhang Shiping said, "We have horses. Fifty head, all good ones."

Su Shuang added, "Five hundred taels of silver, a thousand jin of fine iron — take it all. If you're going to do something great, you'll need these."

Liu Bei rose and bowed deep. He did not say much.

With horses, iron, and money, everything accelerated.

Liu Bei found the best blacksmith. He forged himself a pair of twin swords — balanced for attack and defense. Guan Yu commissioned a long blade — the Green Dragon Crescent, eighty-two jin, curved like a new moon. He hefted it, tested the edge, nodded, and gave it another name: Frostfair Blade. Zhang Fei had a eighteen-chi steel spear made, its two-chi head gleaming, the shaft humming when he flicked it.

Each man fitted himself out in full armor.

The recruits who could not fit in the estate buildings pitched tents nearby. By the final count, they had more than five hundred men.

That day, Liu Bei led Guan Yu, Zhang Fei, and five hundred recruits toward Youzhou City.

The man who received them was Zou Jing. He looked the three of them over — homespun clothes, no official rank, no credentials. He said little and took them to see Governor Liu Yan.

Liu Yan sat in the main hall, his hair half white but his eyes sharp. Liu Bei stepped forward, bowed, and gave his name. Liu Yan asked about his family. Liu Bei answered — from Prince Jing of Zhongshan down, generation by generation, he laid it out clearly.

Liu Yan's eyes brightened as he listened.

"You're of the imperial house?"

"I am."

Liu Yan rose, walked down from his seat, and clapped Liu Bei on the shoulder.

"Then you are my nephew."

Five hundred men with no name had just walked through the gates of Youzhou City.
