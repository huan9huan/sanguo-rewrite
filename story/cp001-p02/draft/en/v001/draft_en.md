# A Hero's Sigh

The day the recruitment notice reached Zhuo County, a man stood reading it against the wall.

He read it from top to bottom, then bottom to top. When he finished, he sighed.

Around him, people sold vegetables, hauled loads, led donkeys. Nobody looked twice.

---

His name was Liu Bei. Courtesy name, Xuande.

Trace his bloodline and you could count all the way back to Emperor Jing of Han — his ancestor was Liu Sheng, Prince Jing of Zhongshan. But trace it that far, and there was nothing left but the counting. The family had lost its title generations ago. His father died young. It was just him and his mother.

He wove straw mats and sandals, took them to the market, traded them for coins. Every evening he brought his mother a bowl of hot soup. That was the life.

But everyone in Lousang Village knew there was something strange about the big mulberry tree outside the Liu family home.

It was too tall. Five zhang and more. From a distance, its canopy spread wide like an umbrella — not like a tree, like the canopy of an imperial carriage. A traveler once stopped, looked up at it for a long time, and said, "This house will produce a great man."

When Liu Bei was a boy, he played under that tree with the village children. While the others ran wild, he stood in its shade, pointed at the canopy, and said:

"Someday I'll be emperor, and I'll use this tree as my carriage canopy."

His uncle Liu Yuanqi heard him. After that, he started sending grain and cloth to the Liu household every few weeks. Someone asked him why. He said, "This boy is no ordinary child."

At fifteen, his mother scraped together what she could and sent him away to study. He studied under Zheng Xuan, then Lu Zhi, and sat in the same room as Gongsun Zan. The masters taught. He did not take to it.

Then he came back to Lousang Village and went on weaving sandals.

---

More than ten years passed. Governor Liu Yan posted the recruitment notice.

Liu Bei, now twenty-eight, stood in front of it and read every word. He was tall — seven chi and five cun, with ears that hung to his shoulders, arms so long his hands reached past his knees, and a pale, clean face that looked out of place among the farmers. But right now, he could do nothing.

He read it. Sighed.

Not from fear. Not from doubt. From a fire in his chest with nothing to burn it on.

Behind him a voice exploded:

"A real man should fight for his country — what are you sighing about!"

Liu Bei turned.

The man stood a head taller than him, shoulders stretching his shirt to the seams. His face was like a leopard's — round eyes wide as saucers, a bristling beard, and a mouth that could shake the plaster off the walls.

Liu Bei looked at him for two seconds. A man this strange, you had to ask.

"What's your name?"

"Zhang Fei. Courtesy name Yide. Born right here in Zhuo. I've got some land, run a wine shop, killed my share of pigs. I like making friends — I just watched you sigh at that notice, so what's going on?"

Liu Bei did not waste words.

"My name is Liu Bei. I'm of the Han imperial house. The Yellow Turbans have risen in rebellion. I want to fight them. But I have no money, no men, no soldiers."

Zhang Fei's eyes lit up.

"I've got money."

Liu Bei looked at him.

"I'll fund it. We recruit. We do this together. What do you say?"

Liu Bei did not answer right away. But something lit in his eyes. He paused, then nodded.

"Come. Let's have a drink first."

The two men turned and walked into a village tavern by the road.

The door stood open, wine jars lined the counter, and sunlight slanted in through the frame. Zhang Fei bellowed for the barkeep to bring wine, loud enough to shake dust from the ceiling.

Liu Bei sat across from this loud-voiced stranger and thought: that sigh was worth it.

The wine had not arrived yet, but the shadow at the door moved first.
