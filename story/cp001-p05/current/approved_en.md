# Heroes and Small Men

By the time they reached Yingchuan, the battle was already over.

They saw it from a distance — the night sky burned orange, the flames lighting up half the horizon. By the time the three brothers led their men into Changshe, the fires had died. Only scorched tents and fields of dead remained.

Huangfu Song and Zhu Jun had struck on a windy night, burning the Yellow Turban camps in a single blaze. Zhang Liang and Zhang Bao fled west with the remnants.

They did not get far.

A force emerged from the darkness, red banners flying, and cut off the road. The man at its head sat seven chi tall, with narrow eyes and a neatly trimmed beard. He was young, but he carried himself differently — still on his horse, unhurried, as if he had come to close the matter.

A cavalry colonel from Qiao County in Peiguo. His name was Cao Cao. Courtesy name Mengde.

Cao Cao planted his five thousand men across the road and gave no speeches. He simply attacked. Ten thousand heads fell. Banners, drums, horses — all seized. Zhang Liang and Zhang Bao barely escaped with their lives. Cao Cao wiped his blade, looked up at the fading smoke on the horizon, and the corner of his mouth moved.

By the time Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei arrived, all of this had happened without them.

They met Huangfu Song and Zhu Jun — two white-haired generals who gave Liu Bei a brief glance and little small talk. Liu Bei passed on Lu Zhi's message.

Huangfu Song said, "Zhang Liang and Zhang Bao are spent. They'll head for Guangzong to join Zhang Jiao. Go back tonight and help your teacher."

Liu Bei took the order. The three brothers turned their fifteen hundred men around and marched back through the night.

---

They pushed hard. Hoofbeats echoed on the dark road for half the night. Near dawn, shapes appeared ahead.

A cluster of soldiers escorting a prison cart.

The cart was wooden, the prisoner inside dressed in gray, his hair half white, his hands bound to the rails.

Liu Bei stopped cold.

He slid from his horse and ran to the cart.

"Teacher —"

Lu Zhi sat inside. He looked up. No surprise. No grief. He simply said:

"I had Zhang Jiao surrounded. We were close to breaking him — but he managed to stall. The court sent a eunuch named Zuo Feng to inspect. He opened his hand and asked for a bribe. I told him we barely had enough grain for the troops, let alone gifts for an imperial envoy."

He paused.

"Zuo Feng went back and told the court I was hiding behind my walls, refusing to fight, demoralizing the army. The court believed him. They sent Dong Zhuo to replace me and had me chained up for trial."

Liu Bei stood beside the cart, silent. His grip on his sword hilt tightened until his knuckles went white.

Behind him, Zhang Fei's hand was already on his spear shaft, his teeth grinding.

"Brother — let me cut down these escorts and get the teacher out —"

"No."

Liu Bei's voice was low but hard.

"The court will sort this out. You will not act rashly."

Zhang Fei's eyes bulged like bronze bells. He stared at Liu Bei for two seconds, then his hand slid off the spear.

The escort did not pause. They pushed the cart forward. Lu Zhi sat upright inside, his back straight. He did not turn his head.

The cart disappeared at the end of the road.

Guan Yu walked over and said, "General Lu has been arrested. Someone else commands the army now — there's no point staying. We should go back to Zhuo."

Liu Bei looked at the road behind, then at the road ahead. A long silence.

"Let's go. Back to Zhuo."

---

They marched north. Less than two days later, the sound of fighting erupted from behind a ridge.

The three men spurred their horses up a hill.

Below — government troops in full rout. Soldiers throwing down helmets and armor, scattering in every direction. Behind them surged a yellow tide, blanketing the hills and valleys. On their banners, four large characters: General of Heaven.

Zhang Jiao.

Liu Bei's gaze swept the field from the ridge. Two words:

"Fast fight."

The three men plunged down the hillside.

Fifteen hundred men followed. Liu Bei said nothing more — Guan Yu peeled left, Zhang Fei right, no instruction needed. Two blades drove into the Yellow Turban mass. Liu Bei charged up the middle with his twin swords.

Zhang Jiao's army had just won a battle and was busy chasing broken troops when this force smashed into their flank. The line buckled, then shattered. They fell back fifty li.

When the Yellow Turbans had vanished into the distance, Liu Bei saw who they had saved.

A thick general sat on his horse, surrounded by a few dozen bodyguards. Fat. His belly strained against his armor. He looked at people with drooping eyelids, as though he were watching ants on the ground.

General Dong Zhuo.

Liu Bei stepped forward and bowed. Dong Zhuo gave him a glance, then asked:

"What is your current rank?"

"No rank. A commoner."

The words were barely out before Dong Zhuo's face changed.

Not anger. Something worse — the expression of a man who suddenly found the conversation beneath him. He flicked his hand, gesturing Liu Bei away. No word of thanks. He turned and walked back to his tent.

Liu Bei stood where he was. He did not move.

Guan Yu's lips pressed into a line. Zhang Fei's face went the color of a liver.

The three men walked out of Dong Zhuo's camp.

Zhang Fei stopped dead.

"We —"

His voice was shaking.

"We fought through blood to save that man. He asks what rank we hold, hears 'commoner,' and won't even look at us!"

He yanked his blade from its sheath.

"I won't rest until I kill him!"

He turned and charged back toward the tent.

Guan Yu reached out — Zhang Fei did not stop.

Liu Bei stood behind, watching Zhang Fei's back. His face was calm.

The blade was already at the tent flap.
