# First Blood

A few days later, word came.

A Yellow Turban commander named Cheng Yuanzhi was marching on Zhuo County with fifty thousand men.

Governor Liu Yan sat in his hall, his face grim. Fifty thousand — Youzhou did not have the troops to fill that hole. He thought for a moment, then called Zou Jing and ordered him to lead Liu Bei and the other two with their five hundred recruits to meet the enemy.

Five hundred against fifty thousand.

Zou Jing said nothing and took the order. Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei said nothing and took the troops.

---

Daxing Mountain.

The two forces met at the foot of the hills.

The Yellow Turbans spread across the valley, dark and endless. Every man had his hair loose, a strip of yellow cloth tied around his forehead, gripping whatever he could find — hoes, hatchets, sharpened sticks, and some proper spears. Not trained soldiers. But there were a lot of them.

Liu Bei sat on his horse, Guan Yu on the left, Zhang Fei on the right. Five hundred men stood behind them in a ragged line.

He did not shout. He did not curse. He glanced at the mass across the field, then at Guan Yu and Zhang Fei.

Guan Yu's hand rested on the hilt of his Frostfair Blade. Steady.

Zhang Fei had his spear raised, the tip pointed forward, taut as a bowstring.

A rider burst from the enemy ranks, spear leveled. Cheng Yuanzhi's lieutenant — Deng Mao.

Liu Bei's gaze shifted from Deng Mao to Zhang Fei. A slight nod.

Zhang Fei moved.

His black horse surged forward, the eighteen-chi spear driving ahead — fast as a snake striking from the grass. Deng Mao's spear had not begun to rise before the point punched through his chest. He toppled from his horse and did not move.

Zhang Fei yanked the spear free and flicked it. Blood scattered across the grass.

Cheng Yuanzhi saw it from the rear. His eyes went red. He spurred his horse, raised his blade, and charged straight at Zhang Fei.

Zhang Fei was about to meet him when a flash of steel swept past from his right.

Guan Yu's horse was faster than expected. The Green Dragon Crescent swept horizontally — Cheng Yuanzhi's blade was still halfway up when the edge caught him across the shoulder and neck.

One cut. Clean through.

Cheng Yuanzhi went down, horse and all.

Guan Yu collected his blade without a glance at the body. He wheeled his horse around, rode back to Liu Bei's side, and rested the halberd across his shoulder.

Fifty thousand men stood frozen.

Their leader was dead. Their lieutenant was dead. A few seconds of silence, and then the retreat began — one man, two men, then a whole wave, then everyone running. Weapons scattered across the ground, yellow headcloths littered the road.

Liu Bei raised his sword and surged forward.

Five hundred men charged after them. They chased for miles. The number of surrenders was beyond counting.

---

Back in Youzhou City, Governor Liu Yan came out to meet them in person.

Wine, meat, silver — all given. Five hundred recruits, their first victory, every face bright.

But good news lasted only two days.

An urgent dispatch arrived at the governor's mansion — Gong Jing, governor of Qingzhou, begging for help. Yellow Turbans had surrounded the city, and the walls were about to fall.

Liu Yan called Liu Bei in and showed him the message.

Liu Bei read it and said four words: "I will go."

Liu Yan nodded. He sent Zou Jing with five thousand soldiers to join the three brothers and march on Qingzhou.

When they reached Qingzhou, fire flickered on the city walls from a distance. The rebel camps spread around the city, tents packed so tight there was no end in sight.

Zou Jing ordered an assault.

The first charge told them everything they needed to know. Too many rebels. Five thousand men surged in, and the enemy swell swallowed them whole. After a hard fight, Liu Bei told Zou Jing to pull back. They fell back thirty li and made camp.

That night, a single oil lamp burned in the command tent.

Guan Yu and Zhang Fei sat to the side, silent. Waiting for Liu Bei to think it through.

Liu Bei stared at the map for a long time. He traced two lines with his finger.

"We're outnumbered. A straight fight won't work. We need surprise."

He looked up at Guan Yu. "Tomorrow, you take a thousand men and hide on the left side of the hills."

Then at Zhang Fei. "You take a thousand and hide on the right."

"Zou Jing and I push from the front and draw them out. When they chase us past the ridge, I sound the gong — and you both hit them from the sides."

Guan Yu nodded. Zhang Fei cracked a grin.

The next day.

Liu Bei and Zou Jing led their troops forward, drums beating, straight toward the Qingzhou walls. The rebels came out to fight. Liu Bei engaged for a stretch, then turned and ran.

The rebels chased.

The moment they crossed the ridge, a gong rang out.

Guan Yu burst from the left, the Green Dragon Crescent sweeping. Zhang Fei burst from the right, the eighteen-chi spear thrusting. Liu Bei turned and charged back in.

Three forces struck at once, like three blades falling together.

Rebels crowded from every direction — no one knew which way to run. The formation collapsed into a rout, driven all the way back toward the city walls. Inside, Gong Jing saw the fighting and led his militia out to join the attack.

The Yellow Turbans scattered completely. Qingzhou was saved.

---

Qingzhou was settled. Zou Jing prepared to march his troops back to Youzhou.

When Liu Bei took his leave, he raised another matter.

"I've heard that the general Lu Zhi is at Guangzong, facing Zhang Jiao's main force. I studied under him once. I'd like to go and lend a hand."

Zou Jing nodded and led his troops home.

Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei took their five hundred men and headed for Guangzong.

After a few days on the road, they reached Lu Zhi's camp.

Tents stretched in long rows. Fifty thousand government troops sat outside Guangzong, and across from them — Zhang Jiao's hundred and fifty thousand. Both sides held. Neither moved.

Liu Bei entered the command tent, bowed, and gave his name and purpose.

A man sat at the head of the tent — hair half white, posture straight, eyes sharp as blades.

Lu Zhi.

He looked at Liu Bei — the face first, then the twin swords, then the red face with the long beard and the leopard eyes next to him. He paused.

"You — Xuande?"

"Teacher."

Lu Zhi stood, walked up to Liu Bei, and clapped him on the shoulder. He did not say much. Two words:

"You're here. Good."

Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei were assigned to the command tent, awaiting orders.

But Lu Zhi's position was not easy. A hundred and fifty thousand Yellow Turbans pressed against Guangzong, and his fifty thousand could only hold. He told Liu Bei: Zhang Jiao's brothers Zhang Liang and Zhang Bao led another force at Yingchuan, facing Huangfu Song and Zhu Jun.

"Take your five hundred men. I'll give you another thousand government troops. Go to Yingchuan, scout the situation, and coordinate the attack."

Liu Bei took the order.

That night, the three brothers left the Guangzong camp with fifteen hundred men and marched toward Yingchuan.

Dark road, long night. Five hundred men from Zhuo County — from the peach orchard to Daxing Mountain, from Daxing Mountain to Qingzhou, from Qingzhou to here.

Their blades were not yet cold. The fighting was not yet done.
