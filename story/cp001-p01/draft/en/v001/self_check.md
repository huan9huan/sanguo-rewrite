## Style Guide Compliance (13_en-style-guide.md)

1. **English readers will keep reading?** Yes — short paragraphs, strong forward momentum, hooks at each section break.
2. **Translation tone?** No "was known as," "is referred to," or explanatory footnotes detected.
3. **Character voice preserved?** The narrator is grim and matter-of-fact. Zhang Jiao is portrayed without glorification. Zou Jing's single line of dialogue is direct.
4. **Hook and ending still work?** Ending: "No one knew yet who it would bring." — retains the Chinese ending's pull.
5. **Cultural terms — no footnote tone?** Eunuchs, governor, court official used without explanation. *Jiazi* in italics, meaning clear from context. Yellow Turbans introduced naturally via apposition.
6. **Names — pinyin, contextualized?** All names in pinyin. Cai Yong = "court official named Cai Yong," Liu Yan = "governor of Youzhou... a man of the imperial Liu clan."
7. **Opening poem treatment?** 3-sentence prose evocation, attributed as "an old song," then cut to story. No line-by-line translation.
8. **Active voice?** Dominates. Passive used only for "Cai Yong was found guilty" (agent genuinely obscure) and "Feng Xu was thrown into prison" (court as agent, standard historical telling).
9. **Reads as originally English?** Yes — sentence rhythms, paragraph breaks, and word choices target native English reading flow.

## Notes

- "Ten zhang" from the Chinese (黑气十余丈) was compressed to "Black vapor pouring straight into the Wende Hall" — avoids a measurement term that would need explanation.
- The *Jiazi* slogan is kept in italics as a block quote to mirror the Chinese treatment. The reader understands it as a prophetic rallying cry from context.
- "The Way of Great Peace" is used for 太平要术 rather than a literal "Essential Arts of Supreme Peace" — reads better as a book title in English narrative.
